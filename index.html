PAPER 1 — SYSTEMATIC REVIEW

Where Is the Evidence?

School-based programs that prevent both dating violence and substance use among adolescents — how many exist worldwide?

I searched 6 databases covering 2012–2025. Out of 5,413 studies screened, only 3 programs in the entire world had been tested with strong research designs. That’s 3 out of 5,413. The prevention field treats violence and substance use as separate problems — but young people experience them together.

Connection to Leiden
This review maps the design principles of effective prevention — directly useful for Prof. Dekker’s T@CKLE project on sexual violence prevention and for building the evidence base this group needs.

THE PARADOX
The program that didn’t target substance use (Green Dot) actually reduced substance-related sexual violence. The program that explicitly targeted it (Fourth R) failed to reduce it. Indirect pathways through norm change may work better than direct instruction.
WHAT THIS MEANS
We need more programs that address violence and substance use together, and we need to design them based on how behavior actually changes — not just what we want to teach.

PAPER 2 — SCHOOL-BASED TRIAL

Changing Norms Takes Years

Does the Green Dot bystander program change how high schoolers think about sexual violence? A 4-year randomized trial.

Connection to Prof. Weenink
Green Dot trains bystanders to intervene — the same dynamic Weenink’s “Circles of Peace” research captures in real-time. My evaluation provides the outcome evidence for the process he observes.
THE QUESTION
At baseline, 30% of students in both groups agreed that a girl who is sexually assaulted while drunk is to blame. Can a bystander program change that?
YEAR 4 RESULTS
Control
21.6%
Treatment
18.8%
Treatment schools showed a 15% reduction in victim-blaming by Year 4. The effect survived strict statistical testing.
KEY FINDINGS
• No difference in Years 1–3. The effect emerged only in Year 4 — lasting change takes sustained effort.
• Boys were 1.7× more likely to endorse victim-blaming than girls.
• Non-White students endorsed at 1.5× the rate of White students — pointing to the need for culturally responsive programming.
• Students who received direct training showed 17% lower endorsement, even after accounting for self-selection.
• Attitudes shifted, but actual reported sexual violence did not yet decrease — norms change before behavior does.
TIMING: 2.5 minutes. Name Weenink. SAY: "Green Dot is a bystander intervention. It trains students to recognize warning signs and intervene. [Point to map] 26 high schools across Kentucky, randomly assigned. Over 16,000 students followed for four years. At the start, almost a third of students in BOTH groups agreed that a drunk girl is to blame for her own assault. [Point to chart] By Year 4, treatment schools were 15% lower. But here's the critical finding: nothing happened for the first three years. The change only appeared in Year 4. That tells us something important about prevention: you can't run a program for one semester and expect lasting change. You have to commit for years. We also found that boys endorsed victim-blaming at nearly twice the rate of girls, and there were racial differences too. And importantly — attitudes shifted, but actual reported violence didn't decrease yet. Norms change before behavior. [Point to Weenink] Professor Weenink, your Circles of Peace work captures how bystanders spontaneously de-escalate violence. My Green Dot evaluation shows what happens when you systematically train them. Together we could connect the situational dynamics to population-level outcomes." PAPER 3 — NATIONAL SURVEY ANALYSIS

Why Opioids? What Makes Them Different?

Three explanations tested with 20,103 U.S. high school students (YRBS 2023)

1.
Risk-taking: teens who use multiple substances and engage in risky behavior also use opioids
2.
Emotional pain: bullied or suicidal teens turn to opioids for relief — because opioids act on the brain’s pain system
3.
Tough circumstances: unsafe homes, sexual victimization, unsafe neighborhoods — the weight of accumulated hardship
Deaths of Despair Link
Opioid misuse co-occurs with suicidal thoughts, dating violence, and bullying — the clustering of despair-driven outcomes. This extends Prof. Liem & Moeller’s work on drugs and violence.
CENTRAL FINDING: BULLYING IS UNIQUE TO OPIOIDS
Bullying was the only risk factor linked specifically to opioid use and not to alcohol, marijuana, cigarettes, or cocaine. Social rejection may activate the same brain pathways that opioids target.

ETHNIC DIFFERENCES MATTER
• Latino/a youth: higher opioid use driven by tough life circumstances — family stress, community instability, acculturative pressure
• White youth: more driven by prescription access, less tied to adversity
• Black youth: lower opioid use overall, but when present, driven by emotional distress
• Multiracial youth: highest rates overall — identity-related stress as a pathway
• All three explanations overlap — these aren’t separate groups of at-risk youth
TIMING: 2.5 minutes. SAY: "This study asks: why do some teenagers turn to opioids specifically, and not other substances? I tested three explanations with 20,000 U.S. high schoolers. [Point to list] Risk-taking, emotional pain, and tough life circumstances. All three mattered. But bullying stood out: it was the ONLY factor uniquely connected to opioid use and not to any other substance. Why? Because social rejection activates the same brain pathways that opioids act on — being excluded literally hurts, and opioids relieve that pain. [Point to ethnic chart] The story also differs by ethnicity. For Latino youth, life circumstances — family stress, community instability — were the primary driver. For White youth, it was more about prescription access. For Black youth, rates were lower overall, but when opioid use was present, emotional distress was the pathway. And all three explanations overlap in the same young people — this is not about neat categories, it's about accumulated burden. [Leiden connection] This directly extends the drugs-violence framework that Professor Liem and Moeller have developed." TRANSITION: "From understanding risk to building solutions."
